Resources

Axelrod, L., & Hone, K. S. (2006). Affectemes and allaffects: a novel approach to coding user emotional expression during interactive experiences.

This article delves into the challenges and solutions of measuring and analyzing user affect during human-computer interaction (HCI). Axelrod and Hone introduce a novel methodology, involving 'affectemes' and their components, to assess user affect displayed through emotional expressions. The authors provide a thorough rationale for their approach, demonstrate its practical application, and present preliminary evidence supporting its efficacy and reliability. This work marks a significant step forward in understanding and evaluating user emotions in interactive experiences.

Bach, C., & Scapin, D. L. (2010). Comparing inspections and user testing for the evaluation of virtual environments.

This study conducts an experimental comparison of three Usability Evaluation Methods—User Testing (UT), Document-based Inspection (DI), and Expert Inspection (EI)—specifically for Virtual Environments (VEs). The research demonstrates that UT and DI are significantly more effective than EI in evaluating VEs. The study provides a comprehensive analysis of problem coverage, problem overlap, identification impact, and reliability across these methods, highlighting the unique strengths and weaknesses of each. Ultimately, the article suggests DI as a versatile and accessible evaluation tool, whereas UT is portrayed as a more powerful, yet skill-dependent method. EI, however, is deemed less efficient for assessing usability in VEs.

Barr, P., Noble, J., & Biddle, R. (2007). Video game values: Human–computer interaction and games.

This article explores the unique aspects of video games in the context of human-computer interaction (HCI), emphasizing how video games differ fundamentally from other software types. Barr, Noble, and Biddle argue that video games define their own activities and contain intrinsic systems of values that players perceive, adopt, and which ultimately shape their gaming experience. The authors advocate for a holistic approach to studying video games, considering them as software, media, and as games in their own right, to achieve a genuine understanding of video game HCI.

Brockmyer, J. H., Fox, C. M., Curtiss, K. A., McBroom, E., Burkhart, K. M., & Pidruzny, J. N. (2009). The development of the Game Engagement Questionnaire: A measure of engagement in video game-playing.

This study introduces the Game Engagement Questionnaire (GEQ), a new tool for measuring player engagement in video games, particularly in the context of violent gameplay. By providing reliable and quantifiable metrics, the GEQ enhances the understanding of engagement's impact on gaming experiences and lays the groundwork for future research in the field.

González Sánchez, J. L., Gutiérrez Vela, F. L., Montero Simarro, F., & Padilla-Zea, N. (2012). Playability: analyzing user experience in video games.

The focus of this study is 'playability,' a criterion for evaluating the quality of a game's user experience. The findings suggest a strong correlation between a game's playability and its success and longevity. The authors propose using playability as a benchmark for game designers, emphasize the importance of balancing challenge and reward, and advocate for the inclusion of effective feedback mechanisms to enhance the gaming experience.

McAllister, G., & White, G. (2015). Video Game Development and User Experience.

McAllister and White delve into the intricacies of game development and its interplay with User Experience (UX), emphasizing that a game's success hinges on the interaction between its graphics, story, and the user. They advocate for a user-centric approach to game development, highlighting the significance of iterative testing and feedback, and positioning UX as a central component of game development, rather than a peripheral consideration.
